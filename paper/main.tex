\begin{abstract}
Different embedding models map inputs to incompatible vector spaces, forcing costly re-embedding when models are switched or combined. We present \textbf{Relative Anchor Translation (RAT)}, a zero-shot protocol that translates between embedding spaces by comparing similarity profiles to shared anchor points, requiring no training data or learned parameters.

Evaluating 17 models from 9 families---spanning BERT-family encoders, a decoder-based LLM, and a scale series from 22M to 335M parameters---we find that translation accuracy is governed by two continuous, independent factors: \textbf{RDM correlation} ($\rho = 0.425$ with R@1) captures shared representational structure, while \textbf{similarity compression} ($\rho = -0.477$) captures discriminability loss. All 17 models share structure to varying degrees; no binary boundary exists. RAT achieves Recall@1 from 0.2\% to 99.6\% across 272 directed pairs, with a crossover against linear alignment at $K \approx 100$ anchors. We provide a practical decision protocol computable from anchor embeddings alone: estimate expected accuracy, select the optimal method, and configure normalization---without retrieval evaluation. Code: \texttt{pip install rat-embed}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ============================================================

The proliferation of embedding models presents a growing interoperability problem. Organizations accumulate vector databases indexed by one model, only to find that newer or specialized models produce incompatible representations. Switching models requires re-embedding entire corpora---a cost that scales linearly with database size and becomes prohibitive for large-scale systems. Multi-model deployments, where different components use different encoders, face the same barrier: vectors from one model cannot be directly compared with vectors from another.

Existing approaches to embedding space alignment rely on learning a transformation between spaces. Procrustes alignment \citep{conneau2018word} fits an orthogonal mapping from parallel data; knowledge distillation \citep{hinton2015distilling} trains a student to mimic a teacher's outputs. These methods require paired training data, model-specific optimization, and separate transforms for each model pair---costs that grow quadratically with the number of models in a system.

We propose \textbf{Relative Anchor Translation (RAT)}, a zero-shot protocol that translates between arbitrary embedding spaces without any training. The core idea is simple: instead of comparing vectors directly, we compare their \emph{similarity profiles}---vectors of similarities to a shared set of anchor points. When two models encode the same text, their patterns of similarity to common reference concepts tend to align, even though their absolute coordinates do not. This transforms the problem from aligning high-dimensional spaces of different dimensions to comparing response patterns in a shared, low-dimensional anchor space.

RAT builds on the relative representation framework of \citet{moschella2023relative}, who showed that cosine similarities to random anchors can enable zero-shot model stitching. We extend this framework in three ways that collectively raise performance from proof-of-concept to practical levels:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Farthest Point Sampling} for anchor selection eliminates the density bias of random anchors, achieving with 100 anchors what random selection requires 1,000 to match (\S\ref{sec:scaling}).
  \item \textbf{Polynomial kernel} similarity $\kappa(u,v) = (u^\top v + 1)^2$ amplifies discriminability at the top of the ranked list, adding +11 points over cosine similarity.
  \item \textbf{DB-side z-score normalization} resolves a previously uncharacterized failure mode---\textbf{similarity collapse}---where compressed similarity distributions in certain model spaces (e.g., multilingual encoders) flatten relative representations and destroy discriminability. This single correction adds +65 points on the most affected pair (\S\ref{sec:collapse}).
\end{itemize}

We evaluate RAT on 17 text embedding models from 9 families, spanning architectures (BERT, XLM-R, CLIP, MPNet, decoder-based LLM), sizes (22M--1.5B), and training objectives (contrastive, distillation, weakly-supervised, instruction-tuned). Across 272 directed pairs, Recall@1 ranges from 0.2\% to 99.6\%. We further extend RAT across modalities using \textbf{Rosetta Stone anchors}---paired (image, caption) concepts that allow a text-only encoder to search a vision encoder's space at Recall@1 = 21.2\%, with zero visual training.

Four main findings emerge from the 17-model evaluation:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{A two-factor model for predicting translation accuracy.} RDM correlation ($\rho = 0.425$ with R@1) and similarity compression ($\rho = -0.477$) independently predict RAT accuracy across 272 pairs. Together they yield a practical decision protocol computable from anchor embeddings alone (\S\ref{sec:diagnostic}).
  \item \textbf{Representational similarity is continuous, not binary.} All 17 models share structure to varying degrees (RDM $\rho \in [0.04, 0.87]$). No hard boundary separates model families; translation accuracy varies continuously with structural similarity (\S\ref{sec:newgen}).
  \item \textbf{Similarity compression dominates within-family accuracy.} Z-score normalization adds up to +88 points on low-compression databases but destroys accuracy ($-$62 points) on compressed ones, diagnosable from sim\_mean alone (\S\ref{sec:asymmetric}).
  \item \textbf{RAT and direct alignment are complementary.} A comparison across 132 pairs reveals a crossover at $K \approx 100$ anchors: RAT dominates with fewer anchors (74\% win rate at $K{=}50$); linear methods dominate with more ($>$90\% at $K{=}500$; \S\ref{sec:alignment}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Relative Anchor Representation}

Let $f_X: \mathcal{T} \to \mathbb{R}^{d_X}$ and $f_Y: \mathcal{T} \to \mathbb{R}^{d_Y}$ be two embedding models mapping inputs to L2-normalized vectors, where $d_X \neq d_Y$ in general. Given a shared set of $K$ anchor texts $\mathcal{A} = \{a_1, \dots, a_K\}$, we define the \emph{relative anchor representation} of an input $x$ under model $X$ as:
\begin{equation}
  r_X(x) = \left[ \kappa(f_X(x), f_X(a_1)), \dots, \kappa(f_X(x), f_X(a_K)) \right] \in \mathbb{R}^K
  \label{eq:relative}
\end{equation}
where $\kappa$ is a kernel function. This transforms vectors from model-specific $d_X$-dimensional spaces into a shared $K$-dimensional space independent of the original dimensions.

We use the polynomial kernel $\kappa(u, v) = (u^\top v + 1)^2$, which outperforms cosine similarity by +11 percentage points on Recall@1 (Table~\ref{tab:kernels} in Appendix). The quadratic nonlinearity amplifies differences among the highest-similarity anchors, improving discrimination at the top of the ranked list.

\subsection{Anchor Selection via Farthest Point Sampling}

Random anchor selection suffers from \emph{density bias}: anchors cluster in high-density regions. We apply Farthest Point Sampling (FPS) to maximize spatial coverage: starting from a random seed, each subsequent anchor is the candidate \emph{most distant} from all previously selected anchors (measured by cosine similarity in a single model's space). This greedy $O(KN)$ procedure runs in seconds on CPU. FPS is executed in Model~A's space; the selected indices are shared across all models.

\subsection{DB-side z-score Normalization}

When anchor inter-similarity is compressed into a narrow range (e.g., E5-large: mean pairwise similarity = 0.72, range = 0.33), relative representations become \emph{flat}, destroying discriminability (\S\ref{sec:collapse}). We apply z-score normalization \textbf{exclusively to the database side}:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{r}_Y(y_j) = \frac{r_Y(y_j) - \mu_j}{\sigma_j}, \quad \mu_j = \frac{1}{K}\sum_k [r_Y(y_j)]_k, \quad \sigma_j = \mathrm{std}(r_Y(y_j))
\end{equation}
The query representation $r_X(x)$ is left unnormalized. DB-side z-score exactly matches both-side normalization across all pairs, while query-side normalization is consistently harmful (\S\ref{sec:asymmetric}).

\textbf{Adaptive application.} Z-score is conditionally beneficial: it helps when the DB model has low similarity compression but \emph{destroys} accuracy on compressed models (\S\ref{sec:asymmetric}). We apply z-score adaptively based on the DB model's sim\_mean (mean pairwise cosine similarity among anchors): apply when sim\_mean $< 0.65$, skip otherwise. This threshold is diagnosable from anchor embeddings alone, requiring no retrieval evaluation.

Algorithm~1 summarizes the complete protocol.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\small
\fbox{\parbox{0.92\columnwidth}{%
\textbf{Algorithm 1:} Relative Anchor Translation\\[3pt]
\textbf{Input:} Query $x$ via model $X$; database $\{y_1,\dots,y_n\}$ via model $Y$; candidates $\mathcal{C}$\\
\textbf{Assumption:} All embeddings are L2-normalized (so $u^\top v = \cos(u,v)$).\\[4pt]
\textbf{Offline} (once per model pair):\\
\quad 1. Encode candidates: $E_X = f_X(\mathcal{C})$\\
\quad 2. Select $K$ anchors via FPS on $E_X$: $\mathcal{A} = \mathrm{FPS}(E_X, K)$\\
\quad 3. Encode anchors: $A_X = f_X(\mathcal{A})$,\; $A_Y = f_Y(\mathcal{A})$\\
\quad 4. Compute DB relative repr.: $R_Y[j,k] = (f_Y(y_j)^\top A_Y[k] + 1)^2$\\
\quad 5. Apply z-score to $R_Y$ (row-wise) $\to \hat{R}_Y$\\[4pt]
\textbf{Online} (per query):\\
\quad 6. $r_X(x) = [(f_X(x)^\top A_X[k] + 1)^2]_{k=1}^K$\\
\quad 7. Retrieve: $\hat{j} = \arg\max_j \cos(r_X(x),\, \hat{R}_Y[j])$
}}
\label{alg:rat}
\end{figure}

\section{Cross-Modal Extension: Rosetta Stone Anchors}
\label{sec:rosetta}
%% ============================================================

For cross-modal retrieval between a text encoder $f_T$ and an image encoder $f_I$, we introduce \textbf{Rosetta Stone anchors}: $K$ concept-aligned pairs $\{(t_k, i_k)\}_{k=1}^K$ where $t_k$ is a caption and $i_k$ the corresponding image (Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}b). Each model encodes its own modality: $r_T(x) = [\kappa(f_T(x), f_T(t_k))]_{k=1}^K$ and $r_I(v) = [\kappa(f_I(v), f_I(i_k))]_{k=1}^K$. Both representations encode the same concept's \emph{response pattern} through different modalities. We use image-caption pairs from COCO Karpathy \citep{lin2014microsoft,karpathy2015deep}, with FPS in the text space.

(b)~Cross-modal: Rosetta Stone anchors provide concept-aligned (caption, image) pairs; each model encodes its own modality.}

\subsection{Setup}

\textbf{Models.} Initial experiments (\S\ref{sec:text}--\S\ref{sec:crossmodal}) use five text encoders: MiniLM (384d, A), E5-large (1024d, multilingual, B), BGE-small (384d, C), CLIP-text (512d, D), BGE-large (1024d, E); plus CLIP ViT-B/32 image encoder for cross-modal experiments. We then extend to 11 models from 6 families (\S\ref{sec:matrix}, Table~\ref{tab:models}) for the systematic analysis: adding E5-small (F), E5-small-multilingual (G), GTE-small (H), GTE-large (I), MPNet-base (J), and BGE-base (K).

\textbf{Datasets.} STSBenchmark (15K sentences), AllNLI (26K sentences), COCO Karpathy (5K image-caption pairs). We sample 2,000 anchor candidates and 500 non-overlapping queries (seed=42).

\textbf{Protocol.} FPS ($K{=}500$, based on Model~A for 5-model experiments; based on each query model for 11-model matrix) + polynomial kernel + z-score (DB-side, applied conditionally per \S\ref{sec:asymmetric}) + cosine kNN. Random baseline: Recall@1 = 0.2\%.

\subsubsection{Representational Structure Across 17 Models}
\label{sec:rdm_structure}

For each model, we compute a Representational Dissimilarity Matrix (RDM) from 2,000 candidate embeddings using cosine distance, then measure the Spearman rank correlation between every pair of models' RDMs. This captures whether two models impose similar pairwise distance orderings on the same set of texts.

The resulting 17$\times$17 matrix (Figure~\ref{fig:rdm_heatmap}) reveals a \emph{continuous} gradient of representational similarity. Family-internal correlations are strong: E5 models (B, F, G; $\rho = 0.75$--$0.86$), GTE (H, I; $\rho = 0.86$), Arctic (N--Q; $\rho = 0.53$--$0.87$). Cross-family correlations vary widely, from near-zero (B$\leftrightarrow$N: $\rho = 0.10$; G$\leftrightarrow$Q: $\rho = 0.06$) to moderate (C$\leftrightarrow$H: $\rho = 0.74$; E$\leftrightarrow$O: $\rho = 0.54$). The overall range is $\rho \in [0.04, 0.87]$ with mean $0.41$ across 136 off-diagonal pairs. No binary cluster boundary exists; instead, representational similarity is a continuous variable that varies with training recipe, data, and family membership.

\subsubsection{Two-Factor Prediction of Translation Accuracy}
\label{sec:twofactor}

What determines whether a model pair can be translated, and how accurately? Across 272 pairs, two factors emerge as independent predictors (Figure~\ref{fig:rdm_vs_r1}):

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{RDM correlation} ($\rho_\text{Spearman} = 0.425$, $p < 10^{-13}$): higher structural similarity yields higher accuracy. Mean R@1 rises monotonically from 27.6\% (RDM $\rho \in [0, 0.2)$) to 86.9\% (RDM $\rho \in [0.8, 1.0)$).
  \item \textbf{Similarity compression} ($\rho_\text{Spearman} = -0.477$, $p < 10^{-16}$): higher compression in the query model yields lower accuracy. Mean R@1 drops from 79.1\% (sim\_mean $\in [0.2, 0.4)$) to 38.3\% (sim\_mean $\in [0.6, 0.8)$).
\end{itemize}

Critically, these factors are \emph{independent}: conditioning on sim\_mean bins, the RDM effect persists ($\rho = 0.34$--$0.49$ within each bin); conditioning on RDM bins, the compression effect persists. Neither subsumes the other.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig5_rdm_sim_vs_r1_v2.png}
  \caption{Two-factor model of RAT accuracy across 272 directed pairs. Left: RDM correlation vs.\ R@1, colored by query-side sim\_mean. Right: sim\_mean vs.\ R@1, colored by RDM $\rho$. Both factors independently predict accuracy.}
  \label{fig:rdm_vs_r1}
\end{figure}

Comparing with the 11-model analysis (\S\ref{sec:collapse}), where sim\_mean alone achieved $\rho = -0.62$: expanding to 17 models introduces wider variation in RDM correlation, and the two-factor model captures variance that sim\_mean alone cannot explain. The 11-model result remains valid as a special case where all pairs share high RDM correlation ($\rho > 0.2$).

\textbf{Scale within training recipe.} The Arctic series (22M--335M, same training recipe) shows high intra-family RDM ($\rho = 0.53$--$0.87$) and RAT accuracy of 39\%--92\% at $K{=}500$, following the two-factor prediction. Scaling parameters 15$\times$ within the same recipe does not qualitatively change representational structure.

\textbf{Implications for PRH.} All 17 models share representational structure to varying degrees: the lowest cross-family RDM ($\rho = 0.04$) is small but positive, and CKA analysis confirms structural correspondence is significantly above chance for all pairs ($p < 0.01$; see Appendix~\ref{app:correction}). This supports the Platonic Representation Hypothesis \citep{huh2024platonic} as a matter of \emph{degree} rather than a binary architectural divide: convergence is continuous, with the degree of sharing predicted by training recipe similarity.

\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Similarity Compression as the Dominant Factor}
\label{sec:collapse}

The 110-pair matrix reveals that a single property of the embedding space---\emph{similarity compression}---is the dominant predictor of RAT accuracy. We define sim\_mean as the mean pairwise cosine similarity among query embeddings: models with high sim\_mean map all inputs to a narrow angular cone, compressing the similarity distribution and leaving little room for relative profiles to discriminate between inputs.

Figure~\ref{fig:compression} plots max(sim\_mean$_Q$, sim\_mean$_{DB}$)---the more compressed model in each pair---against Recall@1. Among the 110 BERT-family pairs (excluding CLIP), the Spearman rank correlation is $\rho = -0.62$ ($p < 10^{-10}$). This single variable explains the broad structure of the heatmap: pairs involving low-compression models (MiniLM, MPNet; sim\_mean $\approx 0.03$) consistently achieve $>$80\%, while pairs involving high-compression models (E5, GTE; sim\_mean $> 0.69$) degrade to 37--70\% unless paired with a low-compression partner.

\textbf{Extending to 272 pairs.} When all 17 models are included (\S\ref{sec:newgen}), the sim\_mean correlation weakens to $\rho = -0.477$ ($p < 10^{-16}$). This is expected: the 17-model set introduces wider variation in RDM correlation, which acts as an independent predictor ($\rho = 0.425$; Figure~\ref{fig:rdm_vs_r1}, right panel). Within sim\_mean bins, RDM correlation contributes additional explanatory power ($\rho = 0.34$--$0.49$; \S\ref{sec:twofactor}). The 11-model finding remains valid as a special case where RDM variation is limited.

\end{figure}

\textbf{Similarity collapse.} The extreme case of compression is \emph{similarity collapse}: Model~B (E5-large, sim\_mean = 0.72, range = 0.33) produces nearly flat relative profiles that destroy discriminability. Without z-score, B$\times$C achieves only 8.4\%; z-score adds +65 points (Table~\ref{tab:collapse}). Collapse is diagnosable via sim\_mean alone.

\textbf{Family effect.} Same-family pairs (e.g., BGE$\times$BGE, GTE$\times$GTE) achieve 89--98\% Recall@1, consistently above cross-family pairs at comparable compression levels. The regression coefficient for same\_family is significant ($+$9 points, $p < 0.01$) even after controlling for sim\_mean. However, same\_family and same\_training are perfectly collinear in our model set---every same-family pair shares the same training objective and likely overlapping training data---so we cannot separate the contributions of shared architecture, training objective, and data overlap. Notably, BGE-small $\times$ GTE-small achieves 99.4\% despite being cross-family; both use BERT-base with contrastive objectives on web corpora. One possible explanation is that training data overlap drives the family effect, but we cannot verify this with current data---controlled experiments (e.g., identical data with different objectives) would be needed to disentangle architecture, objective, and data contributions.

\textbf{Dimensionality is irrelevant.} Models B and E share 1024d output but differ in architecture (XLM-R vs BERT); their direct cosine similarity averages $-0.02$, yet RAT achieves 55.2\%. Cross-dimension A$\times$B (384 $\times$ 1024d) reaches 84\%. Same-dimension B$\times$E (55\%) $<$ cross-dimension A$\times$B (84\%): what matters is compression level, not dimensional agreement. This provides one possible operationalization of the structural convergence posited by \citet{huh2024platonic}.

\subsection{When Z-score Helps and When It Hurts}
\label{sec:asymmetric}

The 5-model experiments established that DB-side z-score is beneficial (\S\ref{sec:method}). The 110-pair matrix reveals this is only half the story: z-score is \emph{conditionally} beneficial, with its effect determined by the DB-side model's compression level (Figure~\ref{fig:zscore}).

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{figures/d2a_zscore_effect.png}
  \caption{Z-score normalization effect (Recall@1 change in percentage points) vs.\ DB-side sim\_mean. Low sim\_mean DB models benefit dramatically (+60--88 pp); high sim\_mean DB models are harmed ($-$40--62 pp). $\rho = -0.59$.}
  \label{fig:zscore}
\end{figure}

When the DB model has low sim\_mean (MiniLM, MPNet; $\approx$0.03), its relative profiles contain rich discriminative information in a wide range. Z-score rescales this well-spread information, making it accessible to compressed query profiles---hence dramatic improvements (e.g., F$\to$A: 1.0\%$\to$89.0\%, $\Delta$=+88pp). When the DB model has high sim\_mean (GTE, E5; $>$0.69), its profiles are themselves compressed with little discriminative information. Z-score amplifies noise alongside the weak signal, causing collapse (e.g., H$\to$I: 98.4\%$\to$36.8\%, $\Delta$=$-$62pp).

The Spearman correlation between z-score effect and DB sim\_mean is $\rho = -0.59$ ($p < 10^{-11}$). Partitioning at sim\_mean = 0.15: pairs with low-compression DB models gain $+$18 points on average from z-score; pairs with high-compression DB models lose $-$6 points. The practical implication is that z-score should be applied \emph{adaptively}: only when the DB model's sim\_mean is below a threshold, diagnosable from anchor embeddings alone.

\textbf{DB-side sufficiency.} Across all pairs, DB-only z-score exactly matches both-side z-score, while query-side normalization is consistently harmful ($-$8 to $-$10 points on well-spread models). This is because DB-side normalization leverages stable statistics from $n$ database vectors, while a single query vector's row-wise z-score distorts the relative magnitudes that carry discriminative signal.

\subsection{Cross-Modal Analysis}
\label{sec:crossmodal_analysis}

A natural expectation is that CLIP-text and CLIP-image, trained jointly via contrastive loss, should yield highly compatible RAT representations. The opposite holds: CLIP-text $\times$ CLIP-image (D$\times$E) achieves only 11.2\% Recall@1 at $K{=}3000$---\emph{half} of independently trained MiniLM $\times$ CLIP-image (A$\times$E, 21.2\%). We trace this to a structural mismatch between what contrastive alignment optimizes and what RAT requires.

RAT relies on \emph{local distance orderings}: if a text and its corresponding image produce similar patterns of similarity to a shared set of anchors, retrieval succeeds. We quantify this by computing, for each matched text-image pair, the Spearman rank correlation $\rho$ between their $K$-dimensional RAT vectors (Table~\ref{tab:anchor_rho}).

CLIP's contrastive objective optimizes the dot product between matched text-image pairs relative to in-batch negatives---a \emph{global} alignment that does not constrain how individual anchor distances are ordered. Moreover, in-batch negative sampling may actively distort local neighborhoods: pushing apart semantically related but non-matched pairs to maximize contrastive discrimination can rearrange the ordering of nearby points in ways that break the distance patterns RAT relies on. The result is that CLIP-text's similarity profile to caption anchors bears little resemblance to CLIP-image's profile to the corresponding image anchors ($\rho = 0.18$; only 12\% of pairs exceed $\rho > 0.3$). By contrast, MiniLM---trained independently on text-only data---shares stronger local structure with CLIP-image ($\rho = 0.33$; 66\% of pairs exceed $\rho > 0.3$). Why independently trained encoders share more local structure than jointly trained ones is an open question; one hypothesis is that both MiniLM and CLIP-image independently organize semantically similar concepts into nearby regions (consistent with the Platonic Representation Hypothesis), whereas CLIP's contrastive loss actively reshapes the text space to maximize global discriminability at the expense of local structure.

Figure~\ref{fig:buckets} provides further evidence. Partitioning query pairs into quartiles by CLIP native text-image cosine similarity, A$\times$E Recall@1 rises from 14.4\% (bottom quartile) to 29.6\% (top quartile)---RAT partially reflects CLIP's alignment, accessing the same underlying structure through different means. D$\times$E shows no such trend (7--10\% across all quartiles), confirming it captures an independent and weaker signal.

  \label{fig:buckets}
\end{figure}

Notably, RAT also accesses structure that CLIP misses. Among the 125 queries in the lowest CLIP similarity quartile, 47 achieve Recall@5 via A$\times$E RAT; the reverse (CLIP-easy, RAT-hard) occurs in only 3 cases. RAT is not a degraded approximation of CLIP alignment but draws on partially distinct structure.

\section{Diagnostic Model: Predicting Translation Accuracy}
\label{sec:diagnostic}
%% ============================================================

The preceding experiments reveal that embedding space translation accuracy is governed by two continuous factors operating independently. We synthesize these findings into a two-factor diagnostic model that predicts---from anchor embeddings alone---what accuracy to expect.

\subsection{RDM Correlation as a Continuous Predictor}
\label{sec:rdm_predictor}

RDM correlation between model pairs predicts RAT accuracy as a \emph{continuous} variable (Spearman $\rho = 0.425$, $p < 10^{-13}$; \S\ref{sec:twofactor}). Mean R@1 rises monotonically with RDM $\rho$: from 27.6\% ($\rho \in [0, 0.2)$, $N{=}26$ pairs) through 42.3\% ($\rho \in [0.2, 0.4)$, $N{=}110$) and 58.7\% ($\rho \in [0.4, 0.6)$, $N{=}106$) to 86.9\% ($\rho \in [0.8, 1.0)$, $N{=}6$). No binary threshold separates ``translatable'' from ``untranslatable'' pairs; instead, RDM $\rho$ sets a continuous upper bound on expected accuracy.

\subsection{Similarity Compression as an Independent Factor}
\label{sec:compression_factor}

Conditional on RDM $\rho$, similarity compression (sim\_mean) explains additional variance (Spearman $\rho = -0.477$, $p < 10^{-16}$). Within each RDM bin, the compression effect persists ($\rho = 0.34$--$0.49$; \S\ref{sec:twofactor}). Among the 110 BERT-family pairs where RDM variation is limited, sim\_mean alone achieves $\rho = -0.62$ (\S\ref{sec:collapse}).

Sim\_mean also determines method selection: it governs when z-score normalization helps ($\rho = -0.59$ with z-score effect, \S\ref{sec:asymmetric}), which direction of RAT is better (\S\ref{sec:asymmetry}), and the $K$ crossover between RAT and linear methods.

\subsection{Practical Decision Protocol}
\label{sec:flowchart}

The two-factor model yields a concrete protocol for deploying embedding translation (Figure~\ref{fig:flowchart}):

\section{Applications}
\label{sec:applications}
%% ============================================================

The 110-pair evaluation establishes RAT's accuracy profile. We now re-analyze this data through the lens of three practical deployment scenarios, requiring no new computation.

\subsection{Model Update Without Re-indexing}

When upgrading from an old embedding model to a new one, the standard approach requires re-encoding the entire database---a cost that scales linearly with database size. RAT replaces this with encoding only $K$ anchor texts with both models ($K{=}500$ in our experiments).

Among 7 same-family upgrade pairs (BGE-small$\to$base, small$\to$large, base$\to$large; E5 and GTE variants), RAT achieves a mean \textbf{Retention Rate} (RAT Recall@1 / re-index Recall@1) of \textbf{93.6\%}, with all pairs above 88\%. For a 1M-document database, re-indexing with a large model takes $\sim$3.5 hours; RAT requires $\sim$6 seconds (500 anchor encodes + matrix multiplication), a cost ratio of 0.05\%.

\subsection{Lightweight Query Gateway}

Using a small, fast model for query encoding while searching a database built with a larger, more accurate model reduces query latency without rebuilding the database. GTE-small (33M params, $\sim$500 docs/sec) querying a GTE-large (335M params, $\sim$80 docs/sec) database achieves \textbf{98.4\% Recall@1 with 6.2$\times$ query speedup}. Cross-family pairs (e.g., MiniLM$\to$BGE-large) achieve 82.4\% at the same speedup.

\subsection{Multi-Database Unified Search}

Organizations with databases built using different models can search them jointly via RATHub. However, naive concatenation of relative representations fails: score scales differ across models (e.g., GTE's compressed representations produce cosine similarities near 1.0, dominating results from other databases). \textbf{Per-database score normalization}---z-scoring similarity scores within each database before merging---resolves this, achieving Recall@1 = 68.8\% and \textbf{Recall@5 = 95.6\%} across three databases (BGE-large, MiniLM, GTE-small). RRF achieves only 33.4\% Recall@1 due to discarding confidence information. Full comparison of merging strategies appears in Appendix~\ref{app:multidb}.

\section{Comparison with Direct Alignment}
\label{sec:alignment}
%% ============================================================

RAT requires no learned parameters---but is this an advantage or a limitation?  When paired anchor embeddings are available, one can fit a linear transformation directly.  We systematically compare RAT against three direct alignment methods.  The core comparison uses 132 directed pairs within the 11-model BERT-family subset (\S\ref{sec:matrix}); we then extend to all 272 directed pairs across 17 models.

\subsection{Setup}

We compare four methods, all using the same FPS-selected anchor pairs:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{RAT}: polynomial kernel + adaptive z-score (our protocol).
  \item \textbf{Orthogonal Procrustes}: $W^* = \arg\min \|AW - B\|^2$ s.t.\ $W^\top W = I$. Restricted to same-dimension pairs (28 of 132).
  \item \textbf{Ridge Regression}: $W^* = (A^\top A + \lambda I)^{-1} A^\top B$, with $\lambda$ selected per pair from $\{10^{-4}, 10^{-2}, 1, 100\}$ by Recall@1 on the test set.  This oracle $\lambda$ selection represents an \emph{upper bound} on Ridge performance; in practice, $\lambda$ selection requires a held-out validation set, and the optimal $\lambda$ shifts with $K$ (e.g., $10^{-4}$ at $K{=}50$ vs.\ $1.0$ at $K{=}500$), adding tuning overhead that RAT avoids entirely.
  \item \textbf{Affine}: Ridge with a bias term.
\end{itemize}
All methods use anchor pairs for fitting (or RAT encoding) and are evaluated on 500 held-out queries.  Anchor counts $K \in \{10, 25, 50, 100, 200, 500\}$ span the range from extremely data-scarce to abundant.  Three FPS seeds confirm stability (mean std $< 0.9$ percentage points).

The crossover at $K \approx 100$ marks where the linear methods accumulate enough training samples to outperform RAT's parameter-free approach.

\textbf{Extension to 272 pairs.}  The crossover pattern is robust to the 17-model expansion (Table~\ref{tab:d1_main}, bottom block).  Among all 272 pairs, RAT wins 74\% at $K{=}50$ (mean $\Delta{=}{+}6.7$ pp) but only 3\% at $K{=}500$ (mean $\Delta{=}-35.3$ pp).  The crossover remains at $K \approx 100$ (39\% RAT win rate).  Notably, linear methods achieve non-trivial accuracy even on low-RDM pairs: at $K{=}500$, Ridge achieves $\ge$33\% on all 272 pairs, including those with RDM $\rho < 0.2$.  This contrasts with RAT, where low-RDM pairs with high query-side compression achieve $<$1\%.  The advantage of linear methods at high $K$ is \emph{amplified} by the inclusion of diverse model pairs.

The asymmetry is structural: RAT projects into a $K$-dimensional space where $K$ is both the representation dimensionality \emph{and} the information budget; linear methods project into the target model's $d$-dimensional space, using $K$ anchors only as training samples.  At low $K$, the linear methods are underdetermined ($K < d$), while RAT's $K$-dimensional representation is well-conditioned by construction.  At high $K$, the linear methods converge toward a near-optimal mapping, while RAT's representation accumulates redundant anchors that dilute discriminability (confirmed by scaling experiments showing performance \emph{declining} at $K{=}2000$ for representative pairs).

\textbf{Hyperparameter dependence.}  Ridge and Affine require selecting $\lambda$, whose optimal value shifts with $K$ (e.g., $\lambda{=}10^{-4}$ at $K{=}50$ vs.\ $\lambda{=}1.0$ at $K{=}500$).  RAT has no such hyperparameter.

\textbf{Dimensionality constraint.}  Procrustes requires $d_X = d_Y$, limiting it to 28 of 132 pairs.  Ridge/Affine handle cross-dimension pairs but are particularly underdetermined when $K \ll d$ (e.g., $K{=}50$ anchors for a $384{\to}1024$ mapping).  RAT handles all pairs uniformly.

\subsection{Directional Asymmetry}
\label{sec:asymmetry}

RAT exhibits pronounced directional asymmetry: the same model pair can yield vastly different accuracy depending on which model's space is used for FPS anchor selection (mean asymmetry = 27.8 pp, max = 96.8 pp at $K{=}500$ across 136 undirected pairs).  Linear methods show much smaller asymmetry (Procrustes: 15.5 pp across 33 same-dimension pairs), because the learned transformation is approximately direction-agnostic.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/d1_simmean_scatter.png}
  \caption{Directional asymmetry is driven by similarity compression.  Each undirected pair contributes two points: the better direction (blue) and worse direction (red).  Points in the harmful zone (sim\_mean $\ge 0.65$, right of dashed line) cluster near 0\% Recall@1.}
  \label{fig:d1_scatter}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:d1_scatter} reveals the cause: when FPS runs in a compressed space (sim\_mean $\ge 0.65$), anchor selection degenerates---points are tightly clustered, FPS cannot achieve coverage, and relative profiles become uninformative.  The Spearman correlation between $|\Delta\text{sim\_mean}|$ and asymmetry is $\rho = 0.47$ ($p < 10^{-4}$).

This motivates \textbf{bidirectional evaluation}: run FPS in both model spaces, compute RAT retrieval in both directions, and select the direction with higher accuracy.  Since FPS and RAT encoding run in milliseconds, the computational overhead is negligible.  We denote this oracle selection \textbf{RAT$_\text{auto}$} in Table~\ref{tab:d1_main}.\footnote{RAT$_\text{auto}$ uses oracle direction selection (best of two directions on the test set).  In deployment without a test set, selecting the lower sim\_mean space as the FPS space correctly predicts the better direction 74\% of the time.  A practical deployment would compute RAT retrieval in both directions and select the one with higher mean similarity score, which closely approximates oracle selection.}  RAT$_\text{auto}$ improves mean Recall@1 from 46.0\% to 61.8\% at $K{=}500$ (+15.8 pp), narrowing the gap with linear methods from 27.1 pp to 11.3 pp.

\subsection{Same-Dimension vs.\ Cross-Dimension Pairs}

When dimensions match, linear methods have a structural advantage: Procrustes finds an exact orthogonal mapping within the shared dimensionality.  Among same-dimension pairs ($N{=}14$ undirected), RAT$_\text{auto}$ wins 81\% at $K{=}50$ but 0\% at $K{=}500$.

Among cross-dimension pairs ($N{=}52$ undirected), where Procrustes is inapplicable, RAT$_\text{auto}$ wins 72\% at $K{=}50$ (mean $\Delta{=}{+}12.0$ pp over Ridge/Affine) and retains a 12\% win rate even at $K{=}500$.  This is RAT's strongest structural advantage: it handles arbitrary dimension mismatches without modification, while Ridge/Affine must fit a rectangular $d_X \times d_Y$ mapping that is severely underdetermined at low $K$.

\subsection{Practical Guidelines}

The comparison yields concrete deployment advice:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $K \le 50$ anchors: \textbf{use RAT}.  Linear methods are underdetermined and unstable.
  \item $K \ge 200$ anchors: \textbf{use Ridge/Affine}.  Sufficient training data enables near-optimal learned mappings.  Use RAT only if dimensions differ and no hyperparameter tuning is feasible.
  \item $50 < K < 200$: either method is viable; RAT$_\text{auto}$ is competitive and parameter-free.
  \item \textbf{Always}: run FPS in the lower sim\_mean space, or use bidirectional evaluation.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% ============================================================

\textbf{Relative Representations.} \citet{moschella2023relative} introduced the framework of encoding data points as similarity vectors to shared anchors, demonstrating zero-shot model stitching. \citet{garcia2024geometric} propose geometric improvements---anisotropic scaling invariance and topological densification---that improve zero-shot stitching on NLP tasks.  RAT operates within this framework: the core idea is theirs. Our contributions are (1)~showing that anchor selection matters critically (FPS achieves with 100 anchors what random requires 1,000), (2)~identifying similarity collapse as a previously uncharacterized failure mode and resolving it with adaptive z-score (a normalization approach complementary to the geometric corrections of \citealt{garcia2024geometric}), and (3)~extending to cross-modal retrieval via paired anchors.

\textbf{Platonic Representation Hypothesis.} \citet{huh2024platonic} hypothesize that models trained on sufficient data converge toward shared representations. Our 17-model evaluation (\S\ref{sec:newgen}) provides quantitative support: all model pairs share representational structure to varying degrees (RDM $\rho \in [0.04, 0.87]$), with the degree of sharing predicted by training recipe similarity rather than architecture or scale. This is consistent with convergence as a \emph{continuous} phenomenon rather than a binary divide. RAT's cross-model Recall@1 offers a quantitative operationalization of convergence degree, computable from black-box model access alone.

\textbf{Model Stitching and Alignment.} \citet{bansal2021revisiting} train affine stitching layers; \citet{merullo2022linearly} learn linear projections for vision-language bridging; Procrustes alignment \citep{conneau2018word} fits orthogonal maps. \citet{lahner2024direct} systematically compare anchor-based relative representations against direct linear/affine alignment, finding that linear transformations often suffice with enough correspondences. Our comparison (\S\ref{sec:alignment}) extends this analysis to 132 directed pairs across 11 models, identifying a crossover at $K \approx 100$ anchors and characterizing the conditions under which each approach excels.

\textbf{Unsupervised Alignment and Structural Matching.} Gromov-Wasserstein optimal transport \citep{alvarez2018gromov} aligns spaces without parallel data by matching intra-domain distance matrices. \citet{schnaus2025blind} achieve blind matching of vision-language embeddings via QAP on pairwise distances, demonstrating that structural matching can succeed when representations share local distance patterns. Our two-factor model (\S\ref{sec:diagnostic}) suggests that these methods should benefit from higher RDM correlation between source and target models, providing a practical compatibility check.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
%% ============================================================

\textbf{Evaluation scale.} All experiments use 500 queries from English-language datasets. Evaluation on larger benchmarks (e.g., MTEB) and multilingual settings is needed. \textbf{Cross-modal ceiling.} Recall@1 of 21.2\% is a proof of concept, not a practical system; the gap to CLIP's native accuracy (59\%) indicates fundamental limits of zero-shot cross-modal translation. \textbf{RAT scaling ceiling.} RAT performance peaks at $K \approx 200$--500 for text-to-text and degrades beyond: on representative pairs, $K{=}2000$ reduces Recall@1 by 1.6--4.4 pp compared to $K{=}500$.  In contrast, Ridge continues improving monotonically with $K$.  The likely mechanism is noise accumulation in high-$K$ cosine similarity, where redundant anchors dilute the discriminative signal.  For cross-modal retrieval, this effect is more pronounced: at $K{=}3000$, matched text-image pairs have mean RAT cosine similarity of 0.983 vs.\ 0.981 for unmatched pairs---a margin of only 0.002, explaining the peak at $K{=}3000$ and decline at $K{=}4000$ (Table~\ref{tab:crossmodal}). \textbf{Polynomial kernel DC bias.} The kernel $\kappa(u,v) = (u^\top v + 1)^2$ introduces a large constant offset when sim\_mean is high: at sim\_mean = 0.5, the mean output is $(0.5 + 1)^2 = 2.25$ with typical variation of $\pm$0.15, yielding a signal-to-offset ratio of 15:1. For high-compression models, relative representations can have pairwise cosine similarity $>$0.99, making retrieval impossible. Centering the kernel or applying per-vector z-score resolves this, but the current protocol does not detect or correct it automatically. \textbf{Compression--family confound.} Same\_family and same\_training are perfectly collinear in our 11-model set; separating the contributions of shared training data, architecture, and objective requires controlled experiments (e.g., same data with different objectives) that our model selection does not permit. \textbf{Low-RDM pairs.} While no binary ``wall'' exists, pairs with low RDM correlation ($\rho < 0.2$) achieve mean R@1 of only 28\%. Whether nonlinear methods or larger paired datasets can improve on this remains open. \textbf{Data integrity.} An earlier version (v3) reported a representational cluster boundary that was traced to a data alignment error (Appendix~\ref{app:correction}). The corrected analysis shows continuous variation with no hard boundary. This experience motivates embedding integrity checks (hash verification, sanity re-embedding) as standard practice. \textbf{Theory.} We provide no formal proof of why relative profiles align; sufficient conditions remain open.

%% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ============================================================

RAT translates between embedding spaces without training via three composable steps (FPS + polynomial kernel + adaptive DB-side z-score), achieving Recall@1 from 0.2\% to 99.6\% across 272 directed pairs spanning 17 models from 9 families.

Evaluating models ranging from 22M-parameter BERT encoders to a 1.5B-parameter decoder-based LLM, we find that translation accuracy is governed by two continuous, independent factors: \textbf{RDM correlation} (Spearman $\rho = 0.425$ with R@1) captures the degree of shared representational structure between model pairs, while \textbf{similarity compression} ($\rho = -0.477$) captures the loss of discriminability in compressed embedding spaces. All 17 models share representational structure to varying degrees---no binary boundary separates ``translatable'' from ``untranslatable'' pairs. This supports the Platonic Representation Hypothesis as a matter of degree: convergence is continuous, with the extent predicted by training recipe similarity.

This two-factor model yields a practical decision protocol: compute RDM correlation and sim\_mean from anchor embeddings to estimate expected accuracy, select the optimal method (RAT for $K \le 50$; Ridge for $K \ge 200$; Procrustes with labeled pairs), and configure normalization---all without retrieval evaluation. Three application scenarios demonstrate practical value: model upgrades retain 94\% accuracy at 0.05\% of re-indexing cost; lightweight query gateways achieve 98\% accuracy with 6$\times$ speedup; and multi-database unified search reaches Recall@5 = 96\%. The protocol is available as an open-source library (\texttt{pip install rat-embed}).

\section{Data Correction Note}
\label{app:correction}

An earlier version of this paper (v3, Zenodo DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.19401276) reported a ``cross-cluster wall''---a hard representational boundary (RDM $\rho \approx 0$) between BERT-family encoders (models A--K) and newer models (L--Q). This finding was incorrect, caused by a data alignment error described below. All results in the current version (v4) use corrected data.

\textbf{Root cause.} The embedding generation script (\texttt{run\_d2a\_embed\_all.py}) used Python's \texttt{set()} to collect unique sentences from the STS Benchmark dataset. \texttt{set()} iteration order is non-deterministic across Python sessions. The script was executed twice: the first run generated embeddings for models A--K with text ordering $T_1$; the second run regenerated \texttt{texts.json} with a different ordering $T_2$ (due to \texttt{set()} non-determinism), skipped A--K (whose embedding files already existed), and generated L--Q embeddings using $T_2$. The result: A--K embeddings corresponded to $T_1$ while L--Q corresponded to $T_2$, but the file-level correspondence was implicit---no integrity check detected the mismatch.

\textbf{Detection.} The error was discovered during a follow-up CKA analysis (``Wall Diagnostics''). Re-embedding model A with the current library and comparing against the cached \texttt{cand\_A.npy} yielded cosine similarity $\approx 0.02$ (expected: 1.0). Systematic verification showed all A--K embeddings were inconsistent with \texttt{texts.json}, while L--Q matched perfectly. File timestamps confirmed the sequence: \texttt{cand\_A.npy} (19:32:04) through \texttt{cand\_K.npy} (19:36:11), then \texttt{texts.json} (19:36:35), then \texttt{cand\_L.npy} (19:37:35)---indicating \texttt{texts.json} was overwritten between the K and L embeddings.

\textbf{Impact.} All cross-group comparisons (A--K vs.\ L--Q) in v3 were invalid: RDM $\rho \approx 0$, $k$-NN overlap at random, ARI $\approx 0$, and zero-shot/few-shot translation failures were artifacts of comparing embeddings of different texts. Within-group results (A--K internal, L--Q internal) were unaffected, as each group used consistent text ordering.

\textbf{Correction.} All 17 models were re-embedded from a deterministic text list (\texttt{sorted(list(set(...)))}) in a single session with no file-skip logic. Verification: for each model, re-embedding \texttt{texts[0]} yielded cosine similarity $\geq 0.9995$ with \texttt{cand\_X[0]}. CKA analysis on corrected data confirms structural correspondence across all model pairs (linear CKA = 0.48--0.75 for former ``cross-cluster'' pairs, all $p < 0.001$).

\textbf{Sections affected in v3.} \S4.6 (cluster discovery, scale invariance), \S6 (cross-cluster wall), \S7 (three-layer model Layer~1). These sections have been rewritten in v4. \S4.2--\S4.5 (110-pair analysis), \S5 (sim\_mean, z-score), \S4.4 (cross-modal), and \S8 (RAT vs.\ alignment, 132 pairs within A--K) were unaffected and are retained with minor updates.

\textbf{Lessons.} (1)~Never iterate over \texttt{set()} when order matters; use \texttt{sorted()}. (2)~Cache-skip logic must verify data integrity (e.g., hash of input texts), not just file existence. (3)~Embed a sanity check at the end of every experiment pipeline: re-embed a few samples and verify cosine similarity $\approx 1.0$ against cached embeddings.
