# §6 Related Work — Draft

**Relative Representations.** Moschella et al. (2023) introduced the framework of relative representations — encoding data points as vectors of similarities to shared anchor points — and demonstrated zero-shot model stitching across vision and language models. RAT operates within this framework: the core idea of anchor-based similarity profiles as a model-agnostic coordinate system is theirs. Our contribution is threefold within their framework. First, we show that anchor selection matters critically: Farthest Point Sampling achieves with 100 anchors what random selection requires 1,000 to match, and polynomial kernels add +11 points over cosine similarity. Second, we identify **similarity collapse** — a previously uncharacterized failure mode where compressed similarity distributions in certain model spaces (notably multilingual encoders) flatten relative representations and destroy retrieval performance. z-score normalization fully resolves this (+50 points), and the finding that DB-side-only normalization suffices simplifies the protocol. Third, we extend the framework to cross-modal retrieval via paired anchors, a setting not explored in the original work.

**Platonic Representation Hypothesis.** Huh et al. (2024) hypothesize that neural networks trained on sufficient data converge toward a shared statistical model of reality, regardless of architecture or training objective. Our finding that models with zero direct cosine correlation (B×E: cos ≈ 0) nonetheless share relative similarity structure (RAT Recall@1 = 55%) provides empirical support for this hypothesis. Moreover, RAT's cross-model Recall@1 offers a quantitative, task-grounded operationalization of convergence degree — a scalar measure computable from black-box model access alone, without requiring internal representations or parallel training data.

**Model Stitching and Alignment.** Several approaches bridge representation spaces through learned transformations: Bansal et al. (2021) train affine stitching layers between intermediate representations; Merullo et al. (2022) learn linear projections for vision-language stitching; Procrustes alignment (Conneau et al., 2018) fits orthogonal maps from bilingual dictionaries. These methods achieve higher precision than RAT on their target pairs but require paired supervision and per-pair optimization. RAT trades peak accuracy for universality: zero learned parameters, CPU execution in seconds, and $O(1)$ additional cost per new model (encode the shared anchors). The approaches are complementary — RAT can serve as a lightweight baseline or initialization for learned alignment.
